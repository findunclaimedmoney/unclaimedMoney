Andrew Robertson, a 75-year-old retired engineer from Melbourne, had almost dismissed the notification as a scam. A letter had arrived suggesting he had unclaimed shares worth thousands of dollars — shares from a company he had worked for in the 1990s and had completely forgotten about.

"First thought? Scam," Andrew said with a chuckle. "But something made me look into it properly before throwing it out."

"I assumed those old shares were worthless. But after searching properly and providing my details, the money was mine. It's given me some welcome extra security in retirement."

What Andrew found, after verifying through the official ASIC Moneysmart register, was $28,450 in unclaimed dividends and shares he had genuinely forgotten about — accumulated from a company he had worked at briefly in the early 1990s before it was acquired by a larger firm.

The Scam Fear Is Real — And Legitimate

Andrew's initial instinct to be suspicious is completely reasonable. Scams targeting people with promises of found money are extremely common. The key difference with legitimate unclaimed money is that you always verify through official government websites — never by clicking links in emails or handing over money upfront.

⚠️ How to Spot a Scam vs. a Legitimate Claim

Legitimate unclaimed money searches are always free. The official register is at moneysmart.gov.au — you search yourself, for free. If anyone asks you to pay to access your unclaimed money or asks for your banking details upfront, it is a scam.

The Lesson: Don't Ignore Old Records

Andrew credits a clear step-by-step guide with making the claim process straightforward. Without knowing exactly which documents to gather and which official site to use, he says he would likely have given up — or worse, fallen for a scam version of the same process.

"The claim process was surprisingly simple with the right checklist. That money's given me a nice buffer for retirement. My advice to everyone? Don't ignore those old accounts — take five minutes to search. You could get a lovely surprise just like I did."

Andrew now checks the ASIC register every year as a habit. He has also encouraged his adult children to search, and one found a small unclaimed bank account from a job they had held as a student.

Unclaimed Shares — A Hidden Category

Many Australians don't realise that shares and dividends can also end up as unclaimed money. When a company is acquired, renamed or restructured, shareholder records can become disconnected. Uncashed dividend cheques also accumulate over years and eventually transfer to ASIC.

The ASIC Moneysmart register holds unclaimed money from bank accounts, shares, life insurance and investment accounts — approximately $2.6 billion nationally. There is no time limit on claiming — money sits there indefinitely waiting for its rightful owner.
